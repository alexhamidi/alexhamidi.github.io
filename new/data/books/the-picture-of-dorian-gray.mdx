Dorian Gray is a young man whose portrait ages in his place, freeing him to pursue a life of hedonism and moral corruption without visible consequence. Lord Henry's influence gradually transforms him from innocent to monstrous.

Wilde's writing is incredible - Lord Henry's aphorisms alone are worth the read. Lines like "the only way to get rid of a temptation is to yield to it" and his monologues on youth and beauty are sharp and memorable. The dialogue carries most of the novel and Wilde makes every exchange feel effortless.

The deeper theme is about the separation of appearance from reality, and how the absence of visible consequences enables moral decay. The ending, where Dorian finally confronts the portrait, is the inevitable conclusion of this premise - you can't separate yourself from the effects of your actions forever. A relatively short novel that's stayed with me.
