Voltaire's satire of Leibnizian optimism - the idea that we live in "the best of all possible worlds." Candide is a naive young man whose tutor Pangloss insists everything happens for the best, while the world subjects them to earthquakes, war, slavery, and every other form of suffering imaginable.

The humor is relentless. Every time you think things can't get worse for Candide, they do, and Pangloss keeps finding ways to justify it. Voltaire's real target isn't optimism itself but the intellectual laziness of using philosophy to avoid confronting reality. The ending - "we must cultivate our garden" - is a rejection of grand theorizing in favor of practical, grounded work.

It's only about 100 pages, moves fast, and is genuinely funny despite being written in 1759. The absurdity of the situations Voltaire constructs to demolish Pangloss's philosophy makes his argument more effectively than any direct rebuttal could.
