Plato's attempt to define justice by constructing an ideal city from scratch through a series of dialogues with Socrates. The central argument is that justice in a person mirrors justice in a state - both require each part to fulfill its proper function.

The Allegory of the Cave is the most famous section for good reason - the image of people mistaking shadows for reality and the pain of turning toward the light is one of the most powerful metaphors in philosophy. The tripartite soul (reason, spirit, appetite) is also a useful framework that shows up everywhere once you know to look for it.

Some parts are harder to take seriously today, especially the rigid class system and the censorship of art. But Plato's method of reasoning through dialogue - building up an argument, stress-testing it, and revising - is itself a lesson in how to think carefully. Worth reading at least once to understand the foundation so much Western thought builds on.
