Machiavelli's handbook on acquiring and maintaining political power, written as advice to Lorenzo de' Medici. The central thesis is that a ruler must be willing to act against conventional morality when the situation demands it — it's better to be feared than loved, if you can't be both.

The most interesting parts are the specific historical examples. His analysis of why Cesare Borgia succeeded and then failed, why mercenary armies are unreliable, and how different methods of conquering states lead to different stability outcomes — these are essentially case studies in strategy that still feel relevant.

The book's reputation as purely cynical is somewhat undeserved. Machiavelli isn't advocating cruelty for its own sake — he's arguing that the effective ruler must see the world as it is rather than as he wishes it were. The realism here is closer to what you'd find in a good strategy book than in a philosophy of evil. Short and very readable.
