A tour through the foundations of computer science, quantum mechanics, and the deep connections between them, taught by one of the field's sharpest minds. Aaronson starts from set theory and computability and builds up through complexity classes to quantum computing and quantum information.

The explanations of P vs NP, the role of randomness in computation, and how quantum computers actually differ from classical ones are the clearest I've encountered. He has a gift for distilling what matters about these ideas and cutting through the hype — particularly his point that quantum computers don't just "try all answers at once" but require careful interference patterns to extract useful information.

Some chapters assume more math background than others, and the lecture-note format makes certain sections feel rushed. But the intellectual honesty and willingness to say "we don't know" where appropriate is refreshing compared to most popular science treatments of quantum computing.
