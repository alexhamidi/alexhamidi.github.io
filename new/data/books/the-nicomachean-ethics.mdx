Aristotle's framework for living a good life, centered on the idea that happiness (eudaimonia) comes from exercising virtue in accordance with reason. Virtue is defined as the mean between extremes - courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness, generosity between stinginess and wastefulness.

The doctrine of the mean is elegant and practical. Rather than giving you rigid rules, Aristotle says to develop practical wisdom (phronesis) to judge the right action in each specific situation. His emphasis on habituation - that you become virtuous by practicing virtuous actions repeatedly - connects directly to how we think about skill development and habit formation today.

The sections on friendship are unexpectedly deep. His distinction between friendships of utility, pleasure, and virtue, and his argument that true friendship requires mutual recognition of good character, is one of the most useful frameworks I've found for thinking about relationships. Some of the logic is dense and repetitive, but the core ideas have held up for 2400 years for good reason.
