An introduction to game theory applied to real-world strategic thinking — business, politics, sports, and everyday decisions. Dixit and Nalebuff cover the core concepts: Nash equilibria, credible commitments, signaling, backward induction, and mechanism design, all through practical examples.

The best parts are the specific case studies. Their analysis of the FCC spectrum auctions, the Prisoner's Dilemma in pricing wars, and how to make threats credible by limiting your own options are all concrete enough to actually apply. The chapter on brinkmanship and how to strategically give up control is particularly interesting.

The writing is accessible without being dumbed down, which is hard to do with game theory. Some sections drag when they belabor a point with too many examples, but overall it's the best single book I've found for building intuition about strategic interaction.
