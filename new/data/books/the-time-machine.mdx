A scientist travels to the year 802,701 and discovers humanity has diverged into two species: the childlike Eloi living on the surface and the subterranean Morlocks who farm them. Then he travels even further, to the dying of the Earth itself.

Wells wrote this in 1895 and the central speculation — that class division could literally split humanity into two species — is a remarkably creative extrapolation of Victorian England's social dynamics. The Morlocks maintaining the machinery underground while the Eloi live in blissful ignorance above is an image that's hard to forget.

The far-future scene at the end, with the dimming sun and the desolate beach, is haunting in a similar way to Asimov's _The Last Question_ — both grapple with the ultimate fate of everything. The book is only about 100 pages, and the pacing is excellent throughout.
