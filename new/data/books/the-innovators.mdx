The history of the digital revolution from Ada Lovelace to Google, told through the people who built it. Isaacson traces the lineage of computing through Turing, Von Neumann, the transistor inventors at Bell Labs, the early internet builders, and eventually the personal computer and software pioneers.

The throughline Isaacson emphasizes is that innovation is almost always collaborative rather than the work of lone geniuses. His account of how ARPANET was built by dozens of loosely coordinated researchers, and how the personal computer emerged from the intersection of hobbyists, engineers, and counterculture figures, reinforces this point well.

The coverage is broad, which means some stories get less depth than they deserve — the Bell Labs section in particular feels rushed compared to what _The Idea Factory_ covers. But as a single narrative connecting Lovelace's notes on the Analytical Engine to the modern internet, it's well-structured and gives you a good mental timeline of how all the pieces fit together.
