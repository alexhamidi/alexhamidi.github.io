A collection of essays from scientists across disciplines reflecting on the practice of science itself - the culture, the incentive structures, and how discoveries actually happen versus how they're portrayed.

The strongest essays are the ones that deal with the sociology of scientific communities: how consensus forms, how funding shapes what gets studied, and the gap between the idealized scientific method and the messy reality of lab work and publication. Several contributors are surprisingly candid about the politics involved.

Some essays are more interesting than others, and a few haven't aged well. But the best pieces offer a perspective on science as a human institution that you rarely get from science books focused purely on results.
